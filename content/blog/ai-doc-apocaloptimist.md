**And that's exactly why we keep building.**

I just watched *The AI Doc: Or How I Became an Apocaloptimist*, the new documentary from the filmmakers behind *Everything Everywhere All at Once* and *Navalny*. As someone who builds AI systems for a living, I walked in expecting to feel validated. Instead, I walked out feeling something more interesting: satisfied, but humbled.

## The Experts Don't Have All the Answers Either

Here's what hit me. The documentary features conversations with people at the very top of the AI world: Sam Altman, Dario Amodei, Demis Hassabis, and others shaping the future of this technology. These are the people building the most powerful systems humanity has ever created. And even they wrestle with uncertainty about where this is all heading.

That was a strange feeling for someone who works in this space every day. I build multi-agent systems. I work with LLMs. I think in terms of orchestration layers and model routing. I'd call myself an expert. But watching these leaders grapple with the same questions I have reminded me of something Steve Jobs once said (paraphrased):

> When you grow up, you tend to get told the world is the way it is and your life is just to live inside that world. But life can be much broader once you discover that everything around you was made by people who were no smarter than you.

That realization cuts both ways. It's empowering because it means you can build and shape the future too. But it's also humbling because it means the people steering this ship are figuring it out as they go, just like the rest of us.

## Safety Is Real, but Nobody Has a Crystal Ball

The documentary does a solid job of showing how AI safety testing works today and how it will need to scale exponentially over the next 50 years. The pace of advancement is staggering, and the safety infrastructure has to grow alongside it.

But here's my honest takeaway: we will build safety nets, and they will be imperfect. Nobody can predict every failure mode of a technology that evolves faster than our ability to fully understand it.

And I think that's okay. Not because the risks don't matter, but because that's how every major leap in human history has worked.

## The Challenger Principle

Think about the space program. When NASA sent rockets to the moon, the risks were enormous. The Challenger disaster proved that in the most devastating way possible. People lost their lives. The nation grieved. And then we learned, adapted, and built better systems.

That's the price of innovation. Not that we accept failure casually, but that we accept the reality that pushing boundaries means encountering problems we couldn't have predicted. The key is that we learn from them.

AI is in that same chapter right now. We're building something with world-changing potential, and we're doing it knowing that some things will go wrong in ways we can't foresee. The question isn't whether problems will arise. The question is whether we'll have the courage and the systems in place to respond, adapt, and keep going.

## Why We Keep Building

The potential benefits of AI touch every person on the planet. Medicine, education, scientific discovery, accessibility, creative tools, economic opportunity. The upside isn't abstract. It's practical and it's personal.

I build AI systems because I believe that people who understand this technology have a responsibility to shape it, not just consume it. The documentary calls this mindset being an "apocaloptimist," someone who sees the storm clouds clearly but still believes in the sunlight.

That resonated with me deeply. You can hold both truths at once: this technology is powerful enough to be dangerous, and it's powerful enough to change everything for the better. The people who understand both sides are the ones who need to be at the table.

## Final Thought

If you haven't seen *The AI Doc* yet, go watch it. Whether you're deep in the AI world or just starting to pay attention, it will challenge how you think about what's coming. And if it leaves you feeling a mix of excitement and unease, that means it did its job.

Nobody has a crystal ball. But we all have a choice about whether we sit on the sidelines or help build the future we want to live in.
